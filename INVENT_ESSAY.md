
A video game title is a player's first encounter with a world they have not yet entered. Before a single frame renders, before a controller is picked up or a mouse hovers over "New Game," the title has already begun its work. It sets expectations. It sparks curiosity. It whispers a promise about the experience waiting on the other side. Choosing the right title for a video game is therefore not a trivial act of labeling — it is a foundational creative decision that shapes how a game is discovered, perceived, and remembered.

The purpose of this function is to take a description of a video game — its themes, mechanics, setting, tone, narrative — and rank a set of candidate titles from most fitting to least fitting. The description serves as the anchor, the source of truth about what the game truly is, and each candidate title is evaluated against it. This is not about generating titles from nothing; it is about judging which among several options best serves the game it would represent.

The inputs are straightforward. First, a written description of the game. This might be a brief pitch, a detailed design overview, or anything in between. It tells us what the game is about — its genre, its mood, its setting, its core fantasy. Second, a list of candidate titles. These might come from a brainstorming session among developers, suggestions from a marketing team, or even AI-generated options. The function's job is to bring order to that list by surfacing the strongest choices.

The use-cases are broad. An indie developer working alone might generate a dozen possible names and want a thoughtful outside perspective on which resonates most. A studio might be narrowing down finalists for a game reveal and want to pressure-test their shortlist against the game's identity. A publisher evaluating a portfolio of upcoming releases might want to ensure that each title communicates the right idea to its intended audience. In every case, the function serves the same role: an informed, consistent evaluator that ranks titles based on how well they represent the game they would name.

To rank titles meaningfully, three core qualities must be evaluated. Each captures a different dimension of what makes a title work.

The first quality is thematic relevance. A great title is tethered to the substance of its game. It reflects what the game is actually about — not in a literal, dictionary-definition sense, but in a way that honestly connects to the game's core themes, subject matter, or central conceit. A title for a game about navigating grief through ocean exploration should evoke something of the sea, of loss, of depth. A title for a fast-paced arena shooter should not sound like a meditation app. Thematic relevance asks: does this title belong to this game? Would a player, having finished the game, look back at the title and feel it was the right name all along? A title that is clever but disconnected from the game's substance will mislead players and ultimately feel hollow.

The second quality is tonal resonance. Where thematic relevance concerns what the game is about, tonal resonance concerns how the game feels. Two games might share a theme — say, survival — but feel entirely different. One might be bleak and harrowing, the other lighthearted and comedic. The title must match not just the subject but the emotional register. Consider how differently "Don't Starve" and "The Long Dark" land, despite both being survival games. Each title is pitch-perfect for its respective tone. Tonal resonance asks: does this title prepare the player for the emotional texture of the experience? Does it sound the way the game feels? A mismatch in tone — a whimsical name for a serious game, or a grim name for a playful one — creates dissonance that undermines the player's trust before they even begin.

The third quality is memorability. A title can be relevant and tonally perfect but still fail if it vanishes from the mind the moment someone looks away. Memorability is the quality that makes a title stick — that makes it easy to recall, easy to share in conversation, and easy to search for. This is a function of several subtle factors: rhythm, brevity, uniqueness, and phonetic appeal. Titles that are overly generic disappear into the noise of a crowded marketplace. Titles that are too long or cumbersome resist being spoken aloud. The most memorable titles tend to be distinctive without being alienating, compact without being vague. They have a texture to them — a sonic or visual quality that lodges in memory. Memorability asks: will this title survive in the wild? Will someone who hears it once at a dinner table be able to find it later? Will it stand out on a storefront page among hundreds of other games competing for attention?

These three qualities — thematic relevance, tonal resonance, and memorability — form the evaluative foundation of this function. No single quality is sufficient on its own. A title that is memorable but thematically adrift is just a catchy word with no anchor. A title that is thematically rich but tonally wrong sends mixed signals. A title that is relevant and well-toned but forgettable will never find its audience. The best title is one that scores well across all three dimensions, and it is the interplay between them that this function seeks to measure.

Ultimately, this function exists because naming things is genuinely difficult, and naming a creative work is among the hardest naming tasks of all. A video game title must do an extraordinary amount of work in very few words. By providing a structured, consistent framework for evaluating candidate titles against a game's own identity, this function aims to bring clarity to a decision that is too often made by instinct alone. Instinct matters — but so does rigor, and the best decisions emerge when both are present.
