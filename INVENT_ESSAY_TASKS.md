Evaluate thematic relevance: Examine each candidate title against the game description and assess how well the title connects to the game's core themes, subject matter, setting, and central conceit. A strong title should feel like it belongs to the game — not in a bluntly literal way, but in a way that honestly reflects what the game is about. A player who finishes the game should be able to look back at the title and feel it was the right name. Titles that are clever but disconnected from the game's substance should be ranked lower than titles that are meaningfully tethered to it.

2. Evaluate tonal resonance: Assess how well each candidate title matches the emotional register and mood of the game as conveyed by the description. Where thematic relevance concerns what the game is about, tonal resonance concerns how the game feels. A bleak survival game and a lighthearted survival game demand very different kinds of titles. The title should prepare the player for the emotional texture of the experience — it should sound the way the game feels. Titles that create tonal dissonance, such as a whimsical name for a grim game or a somber name for a playful one, should be ranked lower.

3. Evaluate memorability: Assess how distinctive, compact, and easy to recall each candidate title is. Consider factors like rhythm, brevity, uniqueness, and phonetic appeal. A great title sticks in the mind, is easy to share in conversation, and is easy to search for. Titles that are overly generic, too long, or cumbersome should be ranked lower than titles that are distinctive without being alienating and compact without being vague. The title must be able to survive in the wild — standing out on a crowded storefront page and being findable by someone who heard it mentioned once.
